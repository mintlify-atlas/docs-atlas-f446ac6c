## Overview

The two-layer atmosphere extends the basic climate model with vertical circulation:

**State Variables** (per grid cell):
1. $T$ - Surface temperature (K)
2. $M$ - Surface moisture (kg/m²)
3. $U$ - Upper layer mass (dimensionless, normalized to 1.0)
4. $M_{\text{up}}$ - Upper layer moisture (kg/m²)

Optionally:
5. $T_{\text{up}}$ - Upper layer temperature (K) [Phase 2 feature]

## Physical Mechanisms

### Convective Ascent

Warm, moist air rises from the surface to the upper atmosphere:

**Ascent Rate** (`compute_convective_ascent_rate`):

$$
w_{\text{asc}} = \min\left(f_{\text{lat}} \cdot \frac{\Delta T}{T_{\text{scale}}} \cdot f_{\text{moisture}} \cdot f_{\text{backpressure}} \cdot w_{\text{max}}, \ w_{\text{max}}\right)
$$

where:

**Latitude Factor**:

$$
f_{\text{lat}} = \cos(\text{lat})
$$

(Convection easier in tropics)

**Temperature Excess**:

$$
\Delta T = T_{\text{surf}} - T_{\text{threshold}}, \quad T_{\text{threshold}} = 295 \text{ K (22°C)}
$$

**Moisture Boost**:

$$
f_{\text{moisture}} = 1 + s_{\text{moisture}} \cdot \ln\left(\frac{M_{\text{surf}}}{M_{\text{ref}}}\right), \quad s_{\text{moisture}} = 0.5
$$

(Logarithmic scaling: high moisture enables stronger convection)

**Backpressure Suppression**:

$$
f_{\text{backpressure}} = \frac{U_{\text{mean}}}{U_{\text{mean}} + \max(U_{\text{above}}, U_{\text{floor}})}, \quad U_{\text{mean}} = 1.0
$$

(High mass aloft suppresses further ascent)

**Parameters**:
- $w_{\text{max}} = 5 \times 10^{-5}$ s⁻¹ (maximum ascent rate)
- $T_{\text{scale}} = 20$ K (thermal response scale)
- $M_{\text{ref}} = 5$ kg/m² (reference moisture)
- $M_{\text{min}} = 2$ kg/m² (minimum moisture for deep convection)

**Source Code**: `src/archived/twolayer/atmosphere.jl:66-94`

<Note>
Convection requires THREE conditions simultaneously:
1. Hot surface (T > 295 K)
2. Sufficient moisture (M > 2 kg/m²)
3. Space available aloft (U not too large)

This creates realistic convective patterns: ocean cells with warm water and moisture support strong ascent, while dry deserts suppress convection even when hot.
</Note>

### Subsidence and Descent

Air descends from the upper layer through multiple mechanisms:

**Total Descent Rate** (`compute_total_descent_rate`):

$$
w_{\text{desc}} = U_{\text{above}} \cdot \left(w_{\text{base}} + w_{\text{excess}} + w_{\text{polar}}\right)
$$

**1. Base Subsidence** (always present):

$$
w_{\text{base}} = 5 \times 10^{-6} \text{ s}^{-1}
$$

**2. Mass Excess Descent** (when U > 1):

$$
w_{\text{excess}} = k_{\text{mass}} \cdot \max(0, U - 1) \cdot U, \quad k_{\text{mass}} = 1.5 \times 10^{-4} \text{ s}^{-1}
$$

**3. Polar Sink** (enhanced descent at high latitudes):

$$
w_{\text{polar}} = \begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } |\text{lat}| \leq 60° \\
k_{\text{polar}} \cdot \frac{|\text{lat}| - 60°}{30°} \cdot U & \text{if } |\text{lat}| > 60°
\end{cases}
$$

where $k_{\text{polar}} = 2 \times 10^{-5}$ s⁻¹.

**Source Code**: `src/archived/twolayer/atmosphere.jl:96-140`

<Note>
The polar sink creates a Hadley-like circulation: air rises in the tropics (high solar heating), flows poleward aloft, and descends at high latitudes. This mechanism is critical for heat transport from equator to poles.
</Note>

## Moisture Coupling

### Ascent Moisture Transfer

Rising air carries moisture upward, but most precipitates during adiabatic cooling:

**Lifting Condensation** (`compute_ascent_moisture_transfer`):

1. **Moisture Lifted**:

$$
M_{\text{lifted}} = w_{\text{asc}} \cdot M_{\text{surf}} \cdot f_{\text{lift}}, \quad f_{\text{lift}} = 0.1
$$

2. **Temperature Drop During Ascent**:

$$
T_{\text{LCL}} = T_{\text{surf}} - \Delta T_{\text{lift}}, \quad \Delta T_{\text{lift}} = 30 \text{ K}
$$

3. **Saturation at Altitude**:

$$
M_{\text{sat,LCL}} = M_{\text{ref}} \cdot \exp\left(17 \cdot \frac{T_{\text{LCL}} - 273.15}{273.15}\right)
$$

4. **Moisture Surviving to Upper Layer**:

$$
M_{\text{to\_upper}} = \min\left(M_{\text{lifted}}, \ M_{\text{sat,LCL}} \cdot f_{\text{survive}}\right), \quad f_{\text{survive}} = 0.3
$$

5. **Precipitation During Ascent**:

$$
P_{\text{ascent}} = \max(0, M_{\text{lifted}} - M_{\text{to\_upper}})
$$

**Source Code**: `src/archived/twolayer/atmosphere.jl:165-189`

**Example**: Tropical ocean cell with $T = 300$ K, $M = 10$ kg/m², $w_{\text{asc}} = 4 \times 10^{-5}$ s⁻¹:

1. Lifted: $4 \times 10^{-5} \times 10 \times 0.1 = 4 \times 10^{-5}$ kg·m⁻²·s⁻¹
2. LCL temperature: $300 - 30 = 270$ K
3. Saturation at LCL: $\approx 3.2$ kg/m²
4. Can survive: $3.2 \times 0.3 = 0.96$ kg/m²
5. Precipitation: $4 \times 10^{-5} - 0.96 \times 10^{-5} = 3.04 \times 10^{-5}$ kg·m⁻²·s⁻¹

### Descent Moisture Return

Descending air brings its moisture content back to the surface:

**Descent Moisture Flux** (`compute_descent_moisture_transfer`):

$$
\dot{M}_{\text{desc}} = w_{\text{desc}} \cdot \frac{M_{\text{up}}}{\max(U_{\text{above}}, U_{\text{floor}})}
$$

**Source Code**: `src/archived/twolayer/atmosphere.jl:191-204`

### Descent Drying Effect

Descending air warms adiabatically, suppressing surface precipitation:

**Saturation Multiplier** (`compute_descent_saturation_multiplier`):

$$
M_{\text{sat,eff}} = M_{\text{sat}}(T) \cdot \left(1 + s_{\text{dry}} \cdot \frac{w_{\text{desc}}}{w_{\text{base}}}\right)
$$

where $s_{\text{dry}} = 2.0$ (descent drying scale).

**Physical Effect**: Regions with strong subsidence (e.g., subtropics, rain shadows) have effectively higher saturation thresholds, creating arid conditions even with moderate moisture.

**Source Code**: `src/archived/twolayer/atmosphere.jl:206-219`

## Upper Layer Transport

### Meridional (Poleward) Flow

Mass flows poleward in the upper layer, driven by pressure gradients:

$$
\frac{dU}{dt}\bigg|_{\text{meridional}} = k_{\text{merid}} \cdot \nabla_\text{lat} U
$$

where $k_{\text{merid}} = 2.5 \times 10^{-4}$ s⁻¹ (upper meridional coefficient).

### Zonal (East-West) Flow

Zonal flow includes pressure-driven diffusion plus a westerly bias:

$$
\frac{dU}{dt}\bigg|_{\text{zonal}} = k_{\text{zonal}} \cdot \nabla_\text{lon} U + k_{\text{west}}
$$

where:
- $k_{\text{zonal}} = 5 \times 10^{-5}$ s⁻¹ (zonal pressure flow)
- $k_{\text{west}} = 2 \times 10^{-5}$ s⁻¹ (westerly bias - analogous to Coriolis effect)

**Source Code**: `src/archived/twolayer/constants_twolayer.jl:38-41`

<Note>
The westerly bias creates prevailing westerlies in the upper layer, mimicking jet streams. Combined with surface easterlies (not explicitly modeled but emergent from descent patterns), this produces realistic global circulation.
</Note>

## Upper Layer Temperature (Phase 2)

An extended version tracks upper layer temperature explicitly:

### Heating Mechanisms

**1. Ascent Heat Transfer** (`compute_ascent_heat_transfer`):

$$
\frac{dT_{\text{up}}}{dt}\bigg|_{\text{ascent}} = \frac{w_{\text{asc}} \cdot (T_{\text{surf}} - T_{\text{up}}) \cdot f_{\text{heat}}}{\max(U, U_{\text{floor}})}
$$

where $f_{\text{heat}} = 0.3$ (ascent heat transfer fraction).

**2. Latent Heat Release** (`compute_upper_layer_latent_heating`):

$$
\frac{dT_{\text{up}}}{dt}\bigg|_{\text{latent}} = \frac{L_v \cdot P_{\text{ascent}}}{U \cdot C_{\text{up}}}
$$

where:
- $L_v = 2.5 \times 10^6$ J/kg
- $C_{\text{up}} = 1 \times 10^7$ J·m⁻²·K⁻¹ (upper layer heat capacity)

### Cooling Mechanisms

**1. Radiative Cooling** (`compute_upper_layer_radiative_cooling`):

$$
\frac{dT_{\text{up}}}{dt}\bigg|_{\text{rad}} = -k_{\text{rad}} \cdot (T_{\text{up}} - T_{\text{eq}})
$$

where:
- $k_{\text{rad}} = 2 \times 10^{-6}$ s⁻¹ (radiative cooling rate)
- $T_{\text{eq}} = 240$ K (radiative equilibrium temperature)

**2. Descent Heat Loss** (`compute_descent_heat_transfer`):

$$
\frac{dT_{\text{up}}}{dt}\bigg|_{\text{descent}} = -\frac{w_{\text{desc}} \cdot \Delta T_{\text{adiabatic}} \cdot (w_{\text{desc}} / w_{\text{base}})}{\max(U, U_{\text{floor}})}
$$

where $\Delta T_{\text{adiabatic}} = 30$ K (descent adiabatic warming).

**Source Code**: `src/archived/twolayer/atmosphere.jl:240-314`

## Circulation Patterns

The two-layer model produces realistic atmospheric patterns:

### Hadley-Like Circulation

1. **Tropics (0-30°)**:
   - Strong surface heating → convective ascent
   - Rising air → precipitation (ITCZ analog)
   - Upper layer accumulates mass (U > 1)

2. **Subtropics (30-40°)**:
   - Upper layer mass flows poleward
   - Descent zones → high pressure → arid conditions
   - Descent drying → desert formation

3. **Mid-latitudes (40-60°)**:
   - Transitional zone
   - Zonal westerlies in upper layer
   - Variable precipitation

4. **Polar regions (60-90°)**:
   - Polar sink → strong descent
   - Cold, dry conditions
   - Surface easterlies (implicit)

### Monsoon Dynamics

Seasonal or diurnal heating variations can produce monsoon-like circulations:

- **Summer/Day**: Land heats → ascent over land → moist ocean air advects → heavy precipitation
- **Winter/Night**: Land cools → subsidence → dry conditions

## Why Archived?

The two-layer implementation faces challenges:

1. **Parameter Sensitivity**: Small changes in ascent/descent rates cause instability
2. **Timescale Separation**: Vertical exchange (hours) vs horizontal transport (days)
3. **Mass Conservation**: Ensuring $U$ remains physical requires careful numerical treatment
4. **Tuning Complexity**: 15+ interdependent parameters

Future work may re-enable this with:
- Adaptive timestepping for stiff ODEs
- Better initial conditions
- Simplified parameterizations
