## Overview

The moisture system couples temperature and water vapor through:

1. **Evaporation** from warm ocean surfaces
2. **Clausius-Clapeyron relation** governing saturation capacity
3. **Orographic precipitation** when air rises over mountains
4. **Latent heat coupling** between moisture phase changes and temperature
5. **Terrain-dependent transport** with stronger mountain barriers than heat

## Saturation Moisture

### Clausius-Clapeyron Relation

Warmer air can hold exponentially more moisture:

$$
M_{\text{sat}}(T) = M_{\text{ref}} \cdot \exp\left(\lambda \cdot \frac{T - T_{\text{ref}}}{T_{\text{ref}}}\right)
$$

where:
- $M_{\text{ref}} = 5.0$ kg/m² (saturation at reference temperature)
- $T_{\text{ref}} = 273.15$ K (0°C reference)
- $\lambda = 17.0$ (Clausius-Clapeyron scale factor)

**Source Code**: `src/physics/moisture.jl:12-15`

<Note>
The exponent is clamped to [-10, 10] for numerical stability, preventing overflow at extreme temperatures. This covers the range 150 K to 500 K, well beyond physical conditions on the moon.
</Note>

**Example Values**:

| Temperature | $M_{\text{sat}}$ | Relative to 0°C |
|-------------|------------------|------------------|
| 253 K (-20°C) | 0.46 kg/m² | 9.2% |
| 263 K (-10°C) | 1.45 kg/m² | 29% |
| 273 K (0°C) | 5.00 kg/m² | 100% (reference) |
| 283 K (10°C) | 17.2 kg/m² | 344% |
| 293 K (20°C) | 59.3 kg/m² | 1186% |
| 303 K (30°C) | 204 kg/m² | 4080% |

## Evaporation

### Ocean Surface Evaporation

Evaporation occurs only from ocean cells above threshold temperature:

**Evaporation Rate** (`compute_ocean_evaporation_rate`):

$$
E(T, z) = \begin{cases}
k_{\text{evap}} \cdot (T - T_{\text{evap}}) & \text{if } z < 0 \text{ and } T > T_{\text{evap}} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

where:
- $k_{\text{evap}} = 0.3 \times 10^{-5}$ kg·m⁻²·s⁻¹·K⁻¹ (evaporation rate coefficient)
- $T_{\text{evap}} = 273.15$ K (evaporation threshold - freezing point)
- $z$ = elevation (negative for ocean)

**Source Code**: `src/physics/moisture.jl:27-38`

**Example**: Tropical ocean at 300 K:

$$
E = 0.3 \times 10^{-5} \cdot (300 - 273.15) = 8.1 \times 10^{-5} \text{ kg·m⁻²·s⁻¹}
$$

## Precipitation

### Orographic Precipitation

When air rises over mountains, it cools adiabatically, reducing saturation capacity:

**Temperature at Altitude** (`compute_temperature_at_altitude`):

$$
T_{\text{altitude}}(T_{\text{surf}}, z) = T_{\text{surf}} - \Gamma \cdot \max(0, z) \cdot z_{\text{scale}}
$$

where:
- $\Gamma = 6.5 \times 10^{-3}$ K/m (atmospheric lapse rate)
- $z_{\text{scale}} = 5000$ m (elevation normalization)
- $z \in [-1, 1]$ (normalized elevation)

**Precipitation Rate** (`compute_orographic_precipitation_rate`):

$$
P(M, T, z) = \begin{cases}
k_{\text{precip}} \cdot (M - M_{\text{sat}}(T_{\text{altitude}})) & \text{if } M > M_{\text{sat}}(T_{\text{altitude}}) \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

where $k_{\text{precip}} = 0.002$ s⁻¹ (precipitation rate coefficient).

**Source Code**: `src/physics/moisture.jl:46-71`

<Note>
Orographic precipitation creates rain shadows: moisture-laden air rising over mountains precipitates on the windward side, leaving the leeward side dry. This effect is visible in 2D simulations with prevailing wind directions.
</Note>

**Example**: Air at sea level with 10 kg/m² moisture rises to z = 0.5 (2500 m):

1. Surface temperature: 290 K
2. Temperature at altitude: $290 - 6.5 \times 10^{-3} \times 2500 = 273.75$ K
3. Saturation at altitude: $M_{\text{sat}}(273.75) \approx 5.2$ kg/m²
4. Excess moisture: $10 - 5.2 = 4.8$ kg/m²
5. Precipitation rate: $0.002 \times 4.8 = 0.0096$ kg·m⁻²·s⁻¹

## Latent Heat Coupling

Phase changes of water couple moisture and temperature dynamics:

**Latent Heat Flux** (`compute_latent_heat_flux`):

$$
Q_{\text{latent}} = L_v \cdot (P - E)
$$

where:
- $L_v = 2.5 \times 10^6$ J/kg (latent heat of vaporization)
- $P$ = precipitation rate (kg·m⁻²·s⁻¹)
- $E$ = evaporation rate (kg·m⁻²·s⁻¹)

**Physical Interpretation**:
- $P > E$: Net condensation → releases heat (warms surface)
- $E > P$: Net evaporation → absorbs heat (cools surface)

**Source Code**: `src/physics/moisture.jl:74-81`

<Note>
Latent heat creates a moisture-temperature feedback: warm ocean evaporates → moisture advects → encounters mountains → precipitates → releases latent heat → warms region. This feedback can sustain monsoon-like circulation patterns.
</Note>

## Moisture Transport

### Terrain Barriers

Moisture transport uses similar terrain modifiers as heat, but with **stronger mountain barriers**:

**Moisture Barrier** (`compute_directional_moisture_transport_modifier`):

$$
f_{\text{moisture\_barrier}}(\Delta z) = \frac{1}{1 + s_{\text{moisture}} \cdot (\Delta z)^2}
$$

where $s_{\text{moisture}} = 8.0$ (twice the heat barrier strength of 4.0).

**Moisture Downslope Preference**:

$$
f_{\text{moisture\_asymmetry}}(\Delta z) = 1 - s_{\text{moisture\_down}} \cdot \tanh(\Delta z \cdot s_{\text{moisture\_scale}})
$$

where:
- $s_{\text{moisture\_down}} = 0.3$ (moisture downslope preference)
- $s_{\text{moisture\_scale}} = 3.0$ (moisture slope sensitivity)

**Source Code**: `src/physics/terrain.jl:120-133`

<Note>
Mountains block moisture (barrier strength = 8.0) twice as effectively as heat (barrier strength = 4.0). This creates distinct climate zones: wet windward slopes and dry rain shadows.
</Note>

### Moisture Diffusion

Moisture transport follows a diffusion equation:

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial t}\bigg|_{\text{transport}} = D_M \cdot \nabla \cdot (m_{\text{moisture}} \cdot \nabla M)
$$

where:
- $D_M = 0.7 \times 10^{-4}$ s⁻¹ (moisture diffusion coefficient)
- $m_{\text{moisture}}$ = terrain-dependent transport modifier

## Complete Moisture Budget

For a single grid cell, the moisture equation is:

$$
\frac{dM}{dt} = E(T, z) - P(M, T, z) + D_M \cdot \sum_{\text{neighbors}} m_{ij} \cdot (M_j - M_i)
$$

where:
- $E$ = evaporation (ocean cells only)
- $P$ = orographic precipitation (land cells)
- Transport = diffusion with terrain barriers
